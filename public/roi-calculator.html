How We Calculate These Numbers — Plain Language Methodology

Every figure in this model is sourced from peer-reviewed research published in journals including JAMA, Health Affairs, Lancet, and the Journal of Occupational and Environmental Medicine. Conservative estimates are applied throughout. This is not a guarantee of financial performance.

1. Medical Claims Reduction

We blend four independent peer-reviewed sources. Baicker, Cutler & Song (Health Affairs, 2010) — the gold-standard meta-analysis of 22 wellness programs — provides the primary weight: $3.27 in medical savings per $1 invested. Friedman et al. (MGH/MBSR, 2012) validates with a 43% reduction in total healthcare utilization 12 months post-program. Goetzel et al. (J Occup Environ Med, 2016) adds $2,304 lower annual medical spend per engaged employee. Unutzer et al. (JAMA, 2002) — the landmark IMPACT collaborative care trial — found integrated behavioral health reduced total medical costs by $3,363 per patient over 4 years, applied conservatively to the stress-attributed population.

Weights: Baicker 50% · MGH 25% · Goetzel 15% · Unutzer 10%

2. Absenteeism Reduction

Three sources are blended. Baicker et al. (2010) provides $2.73 in absenteeism savings per $1 invested (55% weight). Hassard et al. (Work & Stress, 2018) — a systematic review — found stress-related absences last 3× longer than physical claims, with 40% of all unplanned absence stress-attributed; we apply a 28% reduction rate consistent with CBT/MBSR literature (35% weight). Hemp (Harvard Business Review, 2004) established a baseline of $603/worker/year in mental-health-driven absence, with structured programs reducing this by approximately 30% (10% validation weight).

Weights: Baicker 55% · Hassard 35% · Hemp 10%

3. Presenteeism Recovery

Four sources inform this driver. Kessler et al. (JAMA, 2006) at Harvard Medical School found stressed employees lose ~27 productive days per year (~7.5% of salary); Aetna's 2015 MBSR outcomes show a 15% productivity recovery (50% weight). Loeppke et al. (JOEM, 2009) — Health and Productivity as Business Strategy — found mental health programs reduce presenteeism by 17–25%; 17% used conservatively (30% weight). Wang et al. (JOEM, 2007) found EAP/mental health programs achieve 6:1 ROI applied as a conservative 3% validation layer (10%). WHO Global Mental Health Report (2022) — $4 return per $1 — provides a final validation weight (10%).

Weights: Kessler/Aetna 50% · Loeppke 30% · Wang 10% · WHO 10%

4. Voluntary Turnover Reduction

SHRM's 2022 Benchmarking Report establishes replacement cost at 75% of annual salary (conservative; some research cites 150%). Harter, Schmidt & Keyes (Gallup meta-analysis, 2020) — spanning 276 organizations — found highly engaged teams show 43% lower voluntary turnover; this is the ceiling applied in our model, scaled by engagement rate. Lyubomirsky et al. (Psychological Bulletin, 2005) — a comprehensive review — found that positive affect (a direct outcome of EQ and mindfulness training) predicts job satisfaction, organizational commitment, and reduced turnover intent. The retention improvement is capped at the Gallup-validated 43% maximum.

Sources: SHRM (2022) · Gallup/Harter (2020) · Lyubomirsky et al. (2005)

5. Workers' Comp BH Comorbidity

NCCI (2021) establishes that WC spend averages 1.5% of total payroll across industries, with 25% of claims carrying a significant behavioral health component. WorkCare Behavioral Health Analysis (2020) found these comorbid claims cost 2–4× more and last 3× longer. Strosahl (2004) — integrated behavioral health research — found early mental fitness intervention reduces primary care and ER utilization by 20–30%. We apply a conservative 50% cost reduction to the BH-comorbid portion of WC claims, capped at 100% of engagement rate to prevent overcounting.

Sources: NCCI (2021) · WorkCare (2020) · Strosahl, Psychiatric Clinics (2004)

Model Design & Conservatism Principles

The engagement rate (from the selected stage) multiplies all driver calculations — higher engagement = proportionally greater savings, but never beyond what the underlying research supports. Behavioral change ramp-up: Year 1 = 45% of full savings, Year 2 = 80%, Year 3 = 100%. Where multiple studies are available, we use weighted blends with the most conservative applicable estimate from each source. All projections are evidence-based estimates; actual results vary by organization, industry, implementation quality, and baseline health status. This model does not constitute a guarantee of financial performance or medical outcomes.

Why VOI Matters as Much as ROI

ROI tells you what happened to the claims data. VOI tells you why it happened — and why it will keep happening.

The Culture-Claims Connection

Research consistently shows that organizational culture is a primary driver of health outcomes. Employees in high-trust, psychologically safe environments have lower cortisol levels, better sleep, stronger immune function, and make fewer ER visits. Culture is not a soft metric — it is a clinical upstream determinant of healthcare utilization. (Sapolsky, 2004; Kivimaki et al., 2012 — Lancet)

EQ Compounds Over Time

Unlike one-off wellness perks, emotional intelligence skills compound. A team member who learns to regulate stress in Year 1 applies that skill to every difficult conversation, leadership challenge, and health decision in Years 2, 3, and beyond. The ROI model captures Year 1-3; the VOI continues indefinitely. This is why Goleman calls EQ "the sine qua non of leadership." (Goleman, Harvard Business Review, 1998)
